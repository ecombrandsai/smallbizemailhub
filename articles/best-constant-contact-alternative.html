Why Small Business Owners Start Looking for Alternatives

Most people who search for a Constant Contact alternative are not unhappy with email marketing itself — they are unhappy with one specific thing: price, complexity, or a feature gap. Before you switch, it is worth naming exactly what is bothering you, because the fix might be simpler than a full platform migration.

The three most common complaints we hear are:

  • Price increased after the trial ended. Constant Contact's entry price rose to $12 per month for up to 500 contacts, which is competitive, but some users were expecting a free tier and were surprised.
  • Feature overload. Some users just want to send a newsletter. They do not need event management, landing pages, or a social scheduling tool. A simpler interface can feel more comfortable.
  • Deliverability questions. A batch of emails landed in spam and the user blamed the platform rather than their list hygiene or subject lines.

The honest answer is that no alternative solves all three problems simultaneously better than Constant Contact does. But this guide will show you exactly where each competitor wins and loses, so you can make a real decision based on your situation rather than a marketing promise.

What Is Actually at Stake When You Pick an Email Platform

Email marketing has the highest return on investment of any digital marketing channel. The data from dozens of industry studies consistently lands in the same range: for every $1 a small business spends on email marketing, it gets back an average of $36. That number is not from a platform's sales page — it comes from aggregated campaign tracking data across retail, services, and hospitality businesses.

That means your platform choice is not just a software decision. It is a revenue decision. Choosing a tool that looks cheaper but has worse deliverability, weaker automation, or a confusing interface can cost you far more in lost sales than you save on the monthly subscription.

Here is a simple way to think about the stakes. Imagine your list has 400 subscribers — mostly past customers who bought from you at least once. If you send one well-timed email — a seasonal sale, a loyalty discount, a new product announcement — and just 2% of those subscribers make a purchase averaging $80 each, that is $640 in revenue from a single email. Your platform cost for the month is $12 to $25 depending on which tool you choose. The math is not close.

The tool you pick should do three things reliably: get your emails into inboxes (deliverability), make it easy to write and send campaigns (usability), and show you what worked (reporting). Every comparison below is judged against those three criteria.

Mailchimp — Best Free-Tier Alternative

Mailchimp's free plan covers up to 500 contacts and 1,000 emails per month. That is enough to get started. The catch is that automation is locked behind the Essentials plan, which starts at $13 per month — more than Constant Contact. The interface has improved significantly over the past two years, but the free plan also adds Mailchimp branding to your emails, which looks unprofessional when you are trying to build trust with customers. Read our direct comparison in the Constant Contact vs Mailchimp head-to-head to see which one wins on seven specific criteria.

MailerLite — Best for Simplicity and Price

MailerLite is the cleanest interface in this list. If your only goal is writing and sending a good-looking newsletter without learning a complex tool, MailerLite is worth a serious look. Its free plan is the most generous of any paid competitor — 1,000 subscribers and 12,000 emails per month with automation included. The trade-off is lighter reporting, no phone support, and a smaller template library. Once you grow past 1,000 contacts, the paid plans start at $9 per month.

Brevo — Best for Transactional Email

Brevo prices by email volume rather than contact count, which is unusual and genuinely useful if you have a large list that you only email occasionally. The free tier sends 300 emails per day with unlimited contacts stored. The platform handles transactional emails (order confirmations, password resets) alongside marketing campaigns in the same dashboard. The downside is that marketing automation is only available on paid plans starting at $25 per month, and the template editor feels dated compared to Constant Contact or Mailchimp.

Klaviyo — Best for E-Commerce Stores

Klaviyo is the most powerful tool on this list if you run a Shopify or WooCommerce store. It pulls real-time data on what customers browsed, added to cart, or purchased, and lets you trigger highly targeted emails based on that behaviour. Abandoned cart sequences, post-purchase upsells, and win-back campaigns are genuinely easy to build. The problem is pricing — it scales steeply, hitting $45 per month at 1,001 contacts and $100 per month at 2,501 contacts. For a general small business that is not primarily e-commerce, that complexity and cost is not justified.

How to Evaluate Any Email Marketing Tool Before You Commit

Switching platforms is annoying. You have to export contacts, rebuild templates, and re-create any automations you set up. To avoid switching twice, do this evaluation process before you sign up for any tool — including the ones on this list.

Step 1: Test the import process with your real list

Export your contacts from wherever they live now — your CRM, a spreadsheet, your old platform — and try importing them into the new tool during the free trial. If the import fails, has errors, or drops custom fields, that is a serious red flag before you have even sent a single email. Every platform claims importing is easy. Test it yourself with your actual data.

Step 2: Build one real automation, not a demo one

Sign up for your own list and trigger the welcome email sequence yourself. Check whether it arrived in your primary inbox or your spam folder. Time how long it took to arrive. If the platform's own test emails are slow or land in spam during a free trial, they will be slower and spammier when you are paying.

Step 3: Contact support with a real question

Ask something specific and slightly unusual — for example, how to exclude subscribers who opened an email in the last 30 days from a re-engagement campaign. The quality and speed of the answer tells you more about ongoing support than any review will. A response that takes 48 hours and does not actually answer the question tells you everything.

Step 4: Check the pricing at your expected list size in 12 months

Do not just check the entry price. Open each platform's pricing page and find the cost at double your current contact count. Email lists grow — that is the point of having one. A platform that costs $9 per month today might cost $39 per month in 12 months if your list grows from 500 to 2,500 contacts. Factor that into the comparison.

Step 5: Send a test campaign to a Gmail and an Outlook address

This is the deliverability test most people skip. Create a Gmail account and an Outlook account specifically for this test. Send a campaign that contains your typical subject line, your logo, a link, and one image. Check where it lands. Primary inbox, promotions tab, or spam. Do this on every platform you trial before you make a final decision.

Which Constant Contact alternative is best for e-commerce stores?

Klaviyo is the strongest e-commerce pick because it pulls live data directly from Shopify or WooCommerce and lets you trigger emails based on browsing and purchase behaviour. Can I switch email platforms without losing my contacts?

Yes. Every major platform lets you export contacts as a CSV file and re-import them into a new tool. The process takes about 20 minutes for a list under 5,000 contacts. You will lose historical open and click data from your old platform, but your subscriber list transfers cleanly. Always export your contact file before you cancel your old account so you do not lose access while the account is still active.

Brevo has a free plan that allows up to 300 emails per day with no contact limit. MailerLite's free plan covers 1,000 subscribers and 12,000 emails per month.
