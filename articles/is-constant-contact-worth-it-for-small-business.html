How to Get the Most Out of Constant Contact as a Small Business

Signing up is the easy part. Getting a real return on your $12 per month requires a few smart habits. Here is a practical playbook based on what we have seen work for small businesses in competitive local markets.

1. Build your list before you send anything. Import every email address you already have — past customers, inquiry leads, event sign-ups. Use Constant Contact's landing page builder to create a simple "Join our list for exclusive offers" page and link to it in your email signature and social bios. Aim for at least 100 contacts before you send your first campaign. A small, engaged list beats a large, cold one every time.

2. Set up a welcome email immediately. This is the single highest-performing email you will ever send. A welcome email typically gets 50–60% open rates compared to 20–25% for regular campaigns. Write one that thanks new subscribers, tells them what to expect, and gives them a reason to come back (a small discount, a helpful tip, or a link to your best content). The Lite plan automates this for you.

3. Send on a consistent schedule. Once a week is ideal for most small businesses. Once every two weeks is acceptable. Less frequent than that and your subscribers forget who you are. Pick a day and time and stick to it. Tuesday through Thursday mornings between 8am and 10am tend to generate the highest open rates across most industries.

4. Write short, specific subject lines. Subject lines under 40 characters consistently outperform longer ones in Constant Contact's own aggregate data. "3 things to know before your appointment" works better than "Hello from [Your Business Name] — we have some exciting updates to share with you this week." Be direct. Treat every subject line like a newspaper headline.

5. Track your numbers and adjust. Log into your Constant Contact dashboard after each send and look at three numbers: open rate, click rate, and unsubscribes. A healthy open rate for small business email is 20–35%. If you are below 20%, experiment with your subject lines. If your click rate is below 2%, make your calls to action clearer and more visible. If you are losing more than 0.5% of your list per campaign, your content may not be relevant enough to your audience.

6. Use the resend-to-non-openers feature (Standard plan). This one feature alone can increase the total reach of any campaign by 20–30%. After 48 hours, Constant Contact automatically resends your email with a different subject line to everyone who did not open the first one. You write the campaign once and get nearly double the impact.

When Constant Contact Might Not Be the Right Fit

Being honest with you is part of our job. Constant Contact is the right call for most small businesses, but not all of them. Here are three situations where a different tool might serve you better.

You have zero budget right now. Constant Contact does not have a permanent free plan. If you genuinely cannot spend $12 per month at this stage of your business, Mailchimp's free plan for up to 500 contacts and 1,000 sends per month is a reasonable starting point. You will trade away phone support and some features, but you will get experience building email habits before you invest money. Come back to Constant Contact when you are ready to scale.

You need sophisticated CRM-style automation. Constant Contact's automation is solid for welcome sequences and basic drip campaigns, but if you need complex branching logic — for example, "if the contact clicked this link but did not buy within 48 hours, send a follow-up, then wait 3 days and send a different message based on their purchase history" — you are looking at ActiveCampaign or Klaviyo territory. These tools cost more and require more setup time, but they are built for that level of complexity.

You send very high email volume at low cost per send. Brevo (formerly Sendinblue) prices by sends per month rather than list size, which makes it more economical if you have a large list but send infrequently, or if you send SMS alongside email. For most small businesses this is not the model that saves money, but it is worth knowing.
