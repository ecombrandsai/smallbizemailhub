Why This Comparison Matters More Than You Think

Most comparison articles treat Constant Contact and Mailchimp like they are basically the same product with different logos. They are not. They were built for different types of businesses, and choosing the wrong one means wasting time on features you do not need while missing the ones you do.

Mailchimp started as a tool for designers and digital brands who wanted full creative control and deep analytics. It has grown into a sprawling marketing platform that now includes websites, landing pages, social ads, and a CRM layer. That sounds great until you realize all those extra features add complexity — and cost — that most small business owners do not need and will not use.

Constant Contact, on the other hand, was built from day one for local businesses: the bakery, the yoga studio, the plumber, the nonprofit. Its tools are simpler, its support is better, and its core email features are more reliable for non-technical users who just want to send a good-looking email to their customer list without spending three hours figuring out the interface.

According to a 2025 Litmus report, email marketing generates an average return of $36 for every $1 spent. That number is only real if the emails actually reach inboxes, the campaigns actually get sent, and you do not give up on the platform after two frustrating weeks. Tool selection has a direct impact on all three.

Head-to-Head: What You Actually Get With Each Tool

Let us go category by category, starting with the things that matter most to a small business owner who needs to send campaigns, grow a list, and see results without becoming a marketing expert.

Pricing

Mailchimp has a free plan, and that is usually the first reason people try it. The free plan allows up to 500 contacts and 1,000 email sends per month. That sounds useful until you hit the ceiling, which happens faster than you expect. Once you cross 500 contacts, Mailchimp's Essentials plan starts at around $13 per month for 500 contacts. As your list grows, the price climbs steeply. At 5,000 contacts, the Essentials plan costs $69 per month. The Standard plan, which includes most of the automation features worth having, starts at $20 per month for 500 contacts and reaches $100 per month at 5,000 contacts.

Constant Contact's Lite plan starts at $12 per month for up to 500 contacts. The Standard plan, which includes automation, A/B testing, and behavioral triggers, starts at $35 per month for 500 contacts. At 5,000 contacts, Standard costs $80 per month. Neither tool is dramatically cheaper than the other at scale, but Constant Contact includes more useful features at every tier for the type of business owner who needs reliability over bells and whistles.

Ease of Use

This is where Constant Contact wins clearly. Its drag-and-drop editor is more intuitive. The campaign creation flow is shorter and more guided. New users typically send their first campaign within 20 minutes of signing up, according to internal Constant Contact onboarding data.

Mailchimp's editor is powerful but cluttered. The platform has added so many features over the years that finding the email builder itself can take a few clicks. If you enjoy exploring software, Mailchimp is satisfying. If you want to build an email in ten minutes and move on with your day, Constant Contact is faster every time.

Templates

Constant Contact offers over 200 professionally designed templates organized by industry: retail, restaurants, nonprofits, professional services, health and wellness, and more. Most look polished and load correctly on mobile without any adjustments. Mailchimp also has a good template library, but many of the best-looking templates require design knowledge to customize without breaking the layout. Constant Contact templates are more forgiving for non-designers.

Automation

Mailchimp's automation capabilities are more advanced on paper. The Standard and Premium plans include multi-step journeys, behavioral triggers, predictive send-time optimization, and detailed journey analytics. If you are running a complex e-commerce funnel with multiple conditional branches, Mailchimp gives you more control.

For most small businesses, those features are overkill. A welcome series, a birthday email, and a re-engagement sequence cover 90% of use cases. Constant Contact's automation handles all three without confusion. The setup process is guided with templates for each common workflow, so you do not need to understand logic branching to get started.

Deliverability

This is the most important metric most comparison articles skip. Deliverability means the percentage of your emails that actually land in the inbox rather than the spam folder or promotions tab. EmailToolTester ran independent deliverability tests throughout 2025 and found that Constant Contact achieved an inbox placement rate of approximately 91.5%, while Mailchimp came in at around 87.2%. Over a list of 2,000 subscribers, that difference means roughly 86 more people see your email each time you send.

Customer Support

Constant Contact offers phone support and live chat on all paid plans, seven days a week. You can call a real person when something goes wrong. This matters enormously for business owners who are not technical and cannot spend hours reading help documentation.

Mailchimp free users get email support for the first 30 days only. After that, free users are on their own with the knowledge base. Paid users get chat and email support, but phone support is only available on the Premium plan, which starts at $350 per month. That is not a realistic option for most small businesses.

Integrations

Both platforms integrate with the major tools small businesses use: Shopify, WooCommerce, Stripe, QuickBooks, Eventbrite, and social media platforms. Mailchimp has a slightly larger integration marketplace with over 300 apps listed. Constant Contact connects with around 450 integrations including popular local business tools like Mindbody, Vagaro, and Lightspeed. For most businesses, both cover the essentials without a meaningful gap.

How to Set Up Your First Constant Contact Campaign in Under an Hour

You do not need a marketing background to run effective email campaigns. Here is a step-by-step approach that works for any small business starting from zero.

Step 1: Import Your Existing Contacts

Before you do anything else, gather every email address you already have. Check your point-of-sale system, your booking software, your Google contacts, your QuickBooks customer list, and your social media followers who have given you their email. Even a list of 200 real customers is more valuable than 2,000 strangers. Import them into Constant Contact as a CSV file. The platform will check for duplicates and format issues automatically.

Step 2: Pick a Template for Your Business Type

In the template library, filter by your industry. Choose a layout that matches what you want to send — a promotion, a newsletter, an announcement. Do not start from a blank canvas. The pre-built templates are designed to perform. Customize the colors to match your brand, add your logo, swap the placeholder text for your actual message, and swap the placeholder image for a real photo. That is the whole design process for most campaigns.

Step 3: Write a Subject Line That Gets Opens

Your subject line determines whether the email gets opened at all. Keep it under 50 characters so it does not get cut off on mobile. Make it specific and personal. "Thank you — here's 20% off your next visit" outperforms "Monthly Newsletter — January 2026" every single time. Use the preview text field to add a second line of context, since most email clients show 40–90 characters of preview text alongside the subject line.

Step 4: Set Up One Automation Before You Launch

Before you send your first campaign, take 15 minutes to set up a welcome email automation. Every time a new subscriber joins your list, they automatically get a welcome message from you. Include a small thank-you offer — 10% off, a free resource, a free consultation call. This single automation typically generates the highest open rates of any email you will ever send, because people read emails from brands they just opted in to. Constant Contact walks you through this setup with a pre-built template.

Step 5: Send and Measure the Right Metrics

After your first send, look at three numbers: open rate, click rate, and unsubscribes. A healthy open rate for a small business list is 20–35%. Click rates of 2–5% are average; above 5% is strong. If your unsubscribe rate exceeds 0.5% on a single send, the content was not relevant or the audience was not expecting it. Adjust your content or frequency and try again. Do not change everything at once — change one variable per campaign so you know what is actually working.

Who Should Actually Choose Mailchimp?

Not everyone belongs on Constant Contact, and it is worth being honest about that. Mailchimp is the better choice in a few specific situations.

You should choose Mailchimp if you run a pure e-commerce business on Shopify or WooCommerce with a large product catalog and you want deep revenue attribution and automated product recommendation emails based on customer purchase history. Mailchimp's e-commerce integrations pull more data and present it more clearly for this use case.

You should also consider Mailchimp if you are a freelancer, creator, or newsletter publisher with a small list who wants a professional-grade tool at no cost and does not mind the limitations. The free plan is genuinely useful for very small lists, and Mailchimp's design tools are excellent for people who care deeply about visual polish.

And if you are a developer or technical marketer who wants access to advanced segmentation, multivariate testing, and a robust API for custom integrations, Mailchimp's Standard and Premium plans offer capabilities that Constant Contact does not match.

For everyone else — local businesses, service providers, nonprofits, consultants, medical and wellness practices, and professional service firms — Constant Contact is the faster, more reliable, and better-supported choice. The trade-off in advanced features is a trade-up in usability and real-world results.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Mailchimp really free for small businesses?

Mailchimp offers a free plan capped at 500 contacts and 1,000 emails per month. Once you grow past that, costs rise quickly. The free plan also removes Mailchimp branding only on paid tiers, and automation features are heavily restricted until you upgrade to Essentials or Standard, starting at around $13 per month.

Which tool has better customer support for beginners?

Constant Contact wins here. Every paid plan includes phone support and live chat, which is rare in this category. Mailchimp free users get email-only support for the first 30 days, then nothing. If you are new to email marketing and want a real person to call when something breaks, Constant Contact is the clear choice.

Does Constant Contact have better deliverability than Mailchimp?

Third-party tests by EmailToolTester consistently show Constant Contact inbox placement rates above 90%, compared to Mailchimp hovering around 86–89%. Over thousands of sends, that gap adds up to real revenue. Better deliverability means more of your customers actually see your emails, not just their spam folder.

Can I switch from Mailchimp to Constant Contact without losing my contacts?

Yes, easily. Export your contacts from Mailchimp as a CSV file, then import that file directly into Constant Contact. The process takes about ten minutes. Constant Contact also offers a free data migration service for businesses with larger lists, and their onboarding team will walk you through it on a phone call.

Which platform works better for local brick-and-mortar businesses?

Constant Contact was built with local businesses in mind. It includes event management tools, coupon features, and a strong library of industry-specific templates for restaurants, salons, retailers, and nonprofits. Mailchimp is more e-commerce and digital-brand focused. If you run a physical store or service business, Constant Contact fits your workflow better.
